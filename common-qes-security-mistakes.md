# Common QES Security Mistakes Organizations Should Avoid

Qualified Electronic Signatures (QES) help organizations establish trust in digital transactions, protect document integrity, and meet regulatory requirements. However, the effectiveness of a QES solution depends not only on technology but also on how it is managed and used.

Many security incidents involving electronic signatures are caused by operational mistakes rather than weaknesses in cryptographic systems. Understanding these common issues can help organizations strengthen their digital signing processes.

## 1. Sharing Signature Tokens

One of the most common mistakes is allowing multiple employees to use the same signature token.

While this may appear convenient, it creates significant security and accountability problems. When several people use the same token, it becomes difficult to determine who actually signed a document.

Each authorized user should have their own certificate and signing device.

## 2. Sharing PIN Codes

A signature token is only as secure as the PIN protecting it.

Organizations sometimes develop informal practices where PINs are shared among team members or written down for convenience. This significantly increases the risk of unauthorized signing activities.

PIN codes should always remain confidential and be known only to the authorized certificate holder.

## 3. Leaving Tokens Connected to Workstations

Some users leave signature tokens permanently connected to their computers.

If the workstation is left unattended, compromised by malware, or accessed by another person, the token may become available for misuse.

Tokens should be connected only when required and removed immediately after use.

## 4. Ignoring Certificate Expiration Dates

Digital certificates have limited validity periods.

Organizations occasionally discover expired certificates only when employees are unable to sign critical documents. Such situations can disrupt business operations and create unnecessary delays.

Maintaining a certificate inventory and monitoring expiration dates helps avoid these problems.

## 5. Failing to Revoke Certificates Promptly

When an employee leaves the organization or changes responsibilities, associated certificates should be reviewed immediately.

Delays in revoking unused certificates may create security risks and complicate access management.

A documented offboarding process should include certificate review and revocation procedures.

## 6. Insufficient User Training

Technology cannot compensate for a lack of user awareness.

Employees often receive signature tokens without proper instruction regarding:

* Secure storage.
* PIN protection.
* Phishing risks.
* Incident reporting procedures.

Regular security awareness training helps reduce human error and improves overall security posture.

## 7. Storing Tokens in Unsecured Locations

Tokens are sometimes left in desk drawers, meeting rooms, or shared office spaces.

Although the cryptographic protection remains intact, physical access increases the risk of theft, misuse, or accidental loss.

Organizations should establish clear policies regarding token storage and handling.

## 8. Treating QES as a Complete Security Solution

A Qualified Electronic Signature is an important security component, but it should not be viewed as a complete cybersecurity strategy.

Organizations should combine QES with additional controls such as:

* Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
* Access management policies.
* Endpoint protection.
* Security monitoring.
* User awareness programs.

Layered security provides significantly stronger protection than any single technology.

## 9. Lack of Incident Response Procedures

Many organizations do not have a documented process for handling lost or compromised tokens.

An effective procedure should define:

* Reporting requirements.
* Certificate revocation steps.
* Replacement processes.
* Business continuity measures.

Preparation allows organizations to respond quickly and minimize disruption.

## 10. Poor Documentation and Asset Tracking

Without proper records, organizations may lose visibility into:

* Issued certificates.
* Token ownership.
* Expiration dates.
* Revocation status.

Maintaining accurate inventories improves governance, compliance, and operational efficiency.

## Conclusion

Most QES-related security incidents result from operational weaknesses rather than failures of cryptographic technology. By implementing clear policies, training users, protecting tokens, and maintaining proper certificate management processes, organizations can significantly reduce risk and increase trust in their digital document workflows.

Qualified Electronic Signatures provide strong security foundations, but their effectiveness ultimately depends on responsible implementation and ongoing management.
